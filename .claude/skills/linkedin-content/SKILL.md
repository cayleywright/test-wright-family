# Writing LinkedIn content that wins in 2026

LinkedIn replaced its old engagement-graph ranking with a single LLM-based ranker ("360 Brew") that reads content semantically and matches it against the viewer's profile. Old tactics (generic quotes, billboard images, engagement-bait, AI-spammed "great post!" comments) are punished. The rules below are what actually moves reach, follows, and revenue now.

Apply these in order. Do not skip steps — pick the smallest applicable subset for the task at hand.

## 1. Lock the ICP and the three topics before writing a word

Before drafting anything, ask (or infer from context) and write down:

- **ICP** — who is the post for? Be specific: "B2B SaaS founders, $1–10M ARR" beats "business owners".
- **Pain point** — one sentence, concrete: "campaigns that don't convert", "cash flow that surprises them at quarter-end".
- **Three core topics** — 80% of all content must sit inside three tightly-related buckets (e.g. for a marketer: digital marketing / OOH / B2B sales). Off-topic posts now actively hurt reach because 360 Brew checks topical relevance against the author's profile.

If the user hasn't given you these, ask once before drafting. Don't guess.

## 2. Hook = the whole game

360 Brew gives the first 1–2 sentences **3–5x more processing attention** than the rest of the post. A weak hook means the post never gets distributed.

A hook must:

- Be on-topic for the author's three core buckets (off-topic hooks tank distribution even if the body is great).
- Promise a specific, concrete payoff — a framework, a number, a counter-intuitive claim.
- Fit in the pre-"see more" preview (~210 chars on desktop, fewer on mobile).
- Avoid: "I want to share...", "Today I learned...", "Here's a thought...", em-dash openers, AI-sounding throat-clearing.

Hook patterns that work:

- **Contrarian claim**: "Personal stories are the worst way to grow on LinkedIn in 2026. Here's what's replacing them."
- **Specific number + curiosity gap**: "I grew from 0 to 1.2M followers using 3 post formats. Two of them barely existed 18 months ago."
- **Named framework promise**: "The SERVE framework turned my cold outreach reply rate from 2% to 17%."
- **Pattern-break observation**: "Your LinkedIn feed is 80% carousels now. That's not an accident."

When rewriting a user's draft, always produce 3 hook variants and label which pattern each uses.

## 3. Default to educational content with a branded framework

Education content outperforms personality/selfie content on saves, dwell time, and follower conversion — and it's far more scalable to produce daily.

Whenever the user has expertise on a topic, **invent or extract a named framework** they can own. Examples to mirror:

- James Clear → "Atomic Habits" + the 1% daily rule (the concept of small habits pre-existed; the naming made it his).
- Simon Sinek → "Start With Why" (purpose/mission existed forever; the framing was new).
- Chris Donlon → SERVE (sales), CreatorOS (content systems).

Framework checklist when drafting:

- Has a short, ownable name (1–3 words, evocative).
- Is an acronym, a number (3 steps, 5 levels), or a metaphor.
- Is *immediately actionable* — the reader can apply it without buying anything.
- Gets repeated across multiple posts so it sticks.

## 4. Format priority: carousels > infographics > video > text-only

In 2026, **carousels and infographics convert the highest into followers** because they force dwell time and saves — both of which 360 Brew weighs heavily. Video gets views but fewer follows.

Default format selection:

| Goal | Format |
|---|---|
| Follower growth | Carousel (8–12 slides) or infographic |
| Authority + saves | Carousel with named framework |
| Reach + awareness | Short-form vertical video (sub-60s) |
| Conversion / case study | Long-form text post (1,200–1,800 chars) |
| Pure text-only | Only if it's a strong narrative or hot take — otherwise reformat |

When the user asks for "a LinkedIn post" without specifying, ask which format, or default to a carousel script if the topic is educational.

### Carousel structure (use this template)

1. **Cover slide** — hook + named framework + visual promise ("The 5-step SERVE framework")
2. **Problem slide** — the pain in one sentence
3. **Why-now slide** — what changed, why this matters in 2026
4. **3–7 step/idea slides** — one idea per slide, big text, minimal words
5. **Example/proof slide** — a case study, a number, a screenshot
6. **Recap slide** — the framework as a single image
7. **CTA slide** — "Save this for later" + soft offer (newsletter / lead magnet)

## 5. Write for depth and dwell time, not for likes

360 Brew rewards time-on-content, not reactions. Optimize for:

- **Length**: 1,200–2,000 characters for text posts; 8–12 slides for carousels.
- **Saves**: explicitly invite saves ("Save this before your next pitch").
- **Substantive comments**: end with a real question, not "Agree?" Replies from the author should be 2+ sentences with new insight — not "Thanks!".
- **Zero engagement bait**: no "comment X for the guide" pods, no AI-generated "Great insight!" reply spam. The algorithm now detects and demotes these.

## 6. Pre-validate every piece against outliers (don't write blind)

Before drafting on a topic, find **3 outlier posts** from creators with a similar ICP to the user's:

- Outlier = a post that got 5–10x more reach than that creator's normal posts.
- Ignore mega-accounts — their lift is from audience size, not content quality. Target creators in the user's follower range +/- 1 order of magnitude.
- Extract the *structure* (hook pattern, framework, format, slide count, CTA), not the words.
- Adapt to the user's voice and ICP. Rename the framework. Add their own proof.

If you can't see the outliers (no web access, no examples provided), say so and ask the user to paste 2–3 high-performing posts from their niche before you draft.

## 7. Build a 3-tier content funnel, not just "posts"

Every content plan should have all three tiers. Don't draft conversion content for someone with no awareness reach, and don't draft only awareness content for someone trying to sell.

| Tier | Goal | Format | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Awareness** (top) | Reach, follows | Hot takes, contrarian observations, trend commentary | 2–3x/week |
| **Consideration** (mid) | Trust, saves, authority | Framework carousels, step-by-step guides, infographics | 2–3x/week |
| **Conversion** (bottom) | Sales | Case studies with numbers, client testimonials, before/afters | 1x/week |

Conversion posts will get the lowest engagement — that's expected. They convert the audience the top two tiers built.

## 8. Always have a deplatforming play (email > LinkedIn)

Algorithms change. Email lists don't. Every account should have a **lead magnet** that gives away genuine value in exchange for an email. The user should mention it in post CTAs, profile features section, and bio link.

Lead magnet rules:

- Feels like it would sell for $20–$200 (a template, a calculator, a Notion OS, a vault of frameworks).
- Solves one painful, narrow problem — not "10 tips".
- Has a clear name that telegraphs the outcome ("The CreatorOS", "The Cold Email Vault").
- Lives on the user's owned domain or newsletter (Kit, Beehiiv, ConvertKit, etc.), not a LinkedIn doc.

When drafting CTAs, default to "Comment [keyword] and I'll DM the [lead magnet]" or "Link in featured section" — never a bare external URL in the post body (LinkedIn down-ranks those).

## 9. Profile optimization (do this before any content work)

If the user's profile doesn't match the content topics, 360 Brew throttles distribution. Audit and fix in this order:

1. **Headline** (220 chars) — formula: `[Role] | I help [ICP] achieve [outcome] through [mechanism]`. E.g. "Founder of Searchable | I help businesses grow traffic and revenue from AI SEO".
2. **About section** — first 3 lines must hook (it truncates). Then credibility, then offer. End with a CTA to the lead magnet.
3. **Featured section** — 2 tiles max, designed like buttons: one for the lead magnet, one for the core offer. Custom thumbnails, not auto-generated.
4. **Banner** — restates the value prop in 6–10 words. No stock photos.
5. **Experience** — current role description should re-state the ICP and outcome (the algorithm reads this).

## 10. Tone and voice rules when drafting

- Short sentences. One idea per line. Heavy line breaks.
- Concrete over abstract: "$47K MRR" beats "growing revenue".
- Specific numbers, named clients (with permission), real screenshots.
- First person, active voice.
- No corporate jargon, no "synergies", no "thrilled to announce", no "humbled".
- No em-dashes. No "—" peppered through the post. Use periods and line breaks instead.
- No emoji bullets unless the user's existing voice uses them. If they do, keep it sparing (1–3 per post max).
- Do not invent stats, client names, results, or quotes. If a number is needed and not provided, leave a `[INSERT NUMBER]` placeholder.

## Working pattern for this skill

When invoked, follow this loop:

1. **Confirm inputs**: ICP, three topics, format, goal (reach/follow/convert), and whether the user has outlier examples. Ask in one batched question if any are missing.
2. **Draft**: produce hook (3 variants), body, and CTA. For carousels, give slide-by-slide copy.
3. **Stress-test**: check it against the rules above — is the hook on-topic? Is there a named framework? Is the CTA save-oriented? Is there a deplatforming play somewhere in the funnel?
4. **Offer the next step**: "Want me to spin this into a 3-post sequence?" or "Want a matching infographic outline?"

Never deliver a draft and a list of caveats. Deliver the draft, then offer one concrete next step.
